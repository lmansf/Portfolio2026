01 / What it was

A reactive market in miniature.

The Owl Park storefront was a real working store backed by Supabase. Real product rows, real prices, real inventory, real cart and checkout flow. Nothing about the front-end was faked.

Behind it sat three n8n agents pretending to be different parts of a live ticket market. A pricing agent watched demand and adjusted prices inside fairness bounds. A restock agent pulled units from a virtual warehouse when stock crossed a threshold. A fair-buyer agent compared prices to nearby comps (Owl Park itself) and bought when the market was favorable.

Every action a visitor took — adding to cart, completing a checkout, abandoning at promo entry — fed back into the agents' state. The storefront reacted to itself.

02 / How it performed

The agents found their rhythm.

The pricing agent settled into a reasonable diurnal pattern within 48 hours — leaning prices up during morning peaks, easing down in the late afternoon as ride passes saturated. Without any explicit time-of-day rules — it just noticed.

The restock agent was the boring one, in a good way. Threshold checks fired on schedule, replenishments completed cleanly, never a stockout during the run.

The fair-buyer was the agent that surprised me. It bought into dips I didn't see coming, then sat patient for two days at a stretch.

— Project notes, day 9

Cart abandonment on the promo-code field stuck out: a third of carts dropped right when the promo input appeared. That's a UX signal, not an agent signal — the visible promo field made people feel they were missing out.

03 / What I learned

Three things I'm taking forward.

Reading the data afterward in Power BI was the part I'd repeat first. Watching the agents in real time was fun, but the post-run dashboard — basket size, abandonment funnel, agent-driven price elasticity — is what made this project useful.

Build the dashboard first, the agents second. Without the data layer, the agents are theater. With it, the agents become a measurable hypothesis.
Fairness bounds are the contract. The pricing agent could have run away into adversarial territory. A simple comp-vs-Owl-Park bound kept it honest, no critic loop needed.
UX signals beat agent signals. The biggest behavioral lever wasn't a clever pricing model — it was where I put the promo-code field. Three months of agent tuning, beaten by a layout choice.
